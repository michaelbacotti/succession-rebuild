1031 Exchange Guide: How to Defer Capital Gains Legally

May 11, 2026

Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code allows real estate investors to defer federal capital gains taxes on qualifying property exchanges. Rather than paying taxes on a gain when you sell an investment property, you reinvest the proceeds into a replacement property of like kind, and the tax obligation moves with the new asset.

Core Requirements

The property being sold and the replacement property must both be held for investment or business use. Personal residences do not qualify. The replacement property must be identified within 45 calendar days of the sale, and the transaction must close within 180 days. The equity from the sold property must be fully deployed — any cash taken out (boot) triggers a taxable event on that portion.

Like-Kind Definition

Courts and the IRS have interpreted like-kind broadly within real estate. Improved land can be exchanged for raw land, a commercial building can be exchanged for a strip mall, and a residential rental can be exchanged for a industrial property. Personal property — equipment, vehicles, furniture — does not qualify under the current rules following the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act.

Timing Traps

The 45-day identification window and 180-day closing window are hard deadlines with no extensions. Failure to identify a replacement property within 45 days disqualifies the exchange entirely. Many investors work with qualified intermediaries who hold sale proceeds in escrow and manage the compliance timeline to avoid missteps.

2026 Considerations

Depreciation recapture rules still apply. When you exchange a property where depreciation was taken, the recapture obligation carries forward to the replacement property. State tax treatment of 1031 exchanges varies — some states do not conform to the federal rules and will tax the gain. Consult a tax professional experienced in real estate exchanges before executing any transaction.

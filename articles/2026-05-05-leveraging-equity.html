Leveraging Equity: Cash-Out Refinances and HELOCs for Investment

May 5, 2026

Using the equity in your existing properties to fund new acquisitions is one of the most common wealth-building strategies in real estate. Two primary tools facilitate this: cash-out refinance mortgages and home equity lines of credit (HELOCs). Understanding how each works, and when to use one versus the other, is a fundamental skill for growing a portfolio.

Cash-Out Refinance

A cash-out refinance replaces your existing mortgage with a new, larger loan and returns the difference to you in cash. For investment properties, the loan-to-value ceiling is typically 75-80%, meaning you can generally access up to 70-75% of the property's appraised value minus the existing loan balance. The interest rate on a cash-out refinance is typically 0.25-0.5% higher than a rate-and-term refinance and runs 0.5-1% higher than primary residence rates.

The key advantage of a cash-out refinance is certainty: you lock in a fixed-rate loan with known payments over a set term. The downside is that you reset the clock on your loan and pay closing costs on the full loan amount.

HELOCs

A HELOC is a revolving line of credit secured by your property's equity, functioning much like a credit card with a variable interest rate. HELOCs typically come with a 10-year draw period followed by a 20-year repayment period. The flexibility is the main advantage — you draw what you need when you need it and only pay interest on the drawn balance. However, the variable rate means payments can increase if the prime rate rises, creating uncertainty in your cash flow planning.

When to Use Each

Use a cash-out refinance when you have a specific acquisition identified and want to lock in long-term fixed financing. Use a HELOC when you want flexibility for multiple future opportunities or want to preserve existing low-rate mortgages that you do not want to pay off. For portfolio investors managing multiple properties, a HELOC often makes more sense as a pre-approved reserve, while a cash-out refinance is the tool for a known, planned acquisition.

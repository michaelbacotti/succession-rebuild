Cap Rates Explained: The Metric Every Investor Should Understand

May 10, 2026

The capitalization rate — commonly referred to as the cap rate — is one of the first metrics investors learn to evaluate income-producing real estate. It provides a quick snapshot of a property's expected return independent of financing structure, making it a useful screening tool when comparing opportunities across different markets and property types.

How to Calculate Cap Rate

The formula is straightforward: divide the property's net operating income (NOI) by its current market value or purchase price.

Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Property Value

NOI is calculated by taking gross rental income and subtracting operating expenses — property taxes, insurance, management fees, maintenance, and vacancy allowance — but not mortgage payments or capital expenditures.

For example, if a property generates $30,000 in NOI and is valued at $400,000, the cap rate is 7.5%.

What a Cap Rate Tells You

Cap rates reflect the relationship between income and price. A higher cap rate generally suggests higher expected return relative to the investment — but it also often signals higher risk or greater vacancy concern. A lower cap rate typically indicates a more stable, in-demand property, though with a lower immediate yield.

Context matters enormously. Cap rates vary significantly by property type, market, and cycle. A 6% cap rate in a major coastal metro might represent a solid core holding, while the same 6% in a tertiary market could indicate overvaluation.

Limitations of Cap Rates

The cap rate is a static snapshot that does not account for financing, tax implications, or future income growth. It also assumes the NOI figure is accurate and stable — not always easy to verify on off-market or value-add deals where the income may be suppressed under current management.

Experienced investors use cap rates as a first screen, then layer in cash-on-cash returns, IRR, and debt service coverage analysis for a complete picture.
